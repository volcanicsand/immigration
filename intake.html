Basic Personal Information

Full Legal Name
Why we ask
Must match exactly what appears on all immigration documents. Any discrepancy — including nickname vs. legal name, maiden name, or translation variations — can cause adjudication delays or denials. 8 CFR § 103.2(b)(1)
Date of Birth
Why we ask
Age determines eligibility for several forms of relief:

Asylum one-year bar does not begin running until age 18 — minors are exempt. INA § 208(a)(2)(E)
CSPA Child Status Protection Act freezes age for derivative beneficiaries; formula depends on exact DOB. INA § 203(h)
Automatic citizenship may have accrued if under 18 when parent naturalized. INA § 320
Country of Birth
Why we ask
Country of birth determines "chargeability" — which per-country annual quota applies to family and employment preference categories. INA § 202(b)

For asylum, country of birth is often (but not always) the country of persecution. Country of citizenship may differ — both are relevant. INA § 208(b)(1)(A)

EB categories: India and China face decades-long backlogs due to per-country caps. Nationals of less-backlogged countries can wait as little as months. INA § 202(a)(2)
Country of Citizenship / Nationality
Why we ask
For asylum, the applicant must demonstrate persecution or a well-founded fear of persecution by the government of their country of nationality, or — if stateless — the country of last habitual residence. INA § 208(b)(1)(B)(i); 8 CFR § 208.13(b)

Dual nationals: if the applicant can access protection in either country of nationality, asylum may be barred unless persecution exists in both. INA § 208(b)(2)(A)(vi); 8 CFR § 208.15
A-Number (if known)
Why we ask
The Alien Registration Number is a unique 9-digit identifier assigned when a person first comes into contact with USCIS or immigration court. Existence of an A-number means prior USCIS contact, prior proceedings, or a prior application. Pull the A-file via FOIA before any hearing — it contains all prior filings and often reveals critical facts (prior applications, old addresses, prior orders) that the client may not remember or may not volunteer.

FOIA requests for A-files: 5 U.S.C. § 552; 6 CFR § 5.3

Entry History

Date of First Entry to United States
Why we ask
Entry date is the starting point for multiple continuous-presence calculations:

Non-LPR Cancellation requires 10 years continuous physical presence. INA § 240A(b)(1)(A)
VAWA Cancellation requires only 3 years continuous presence. INA § 240A(b)(2)(A)(i)
Asylum one-year bar — application must be filed within one year of arrival. Clock starts on entry date (or 18th birthday if entered as minor). INA § 208(a)(2)(B); 8 CFR § 208.4(a)(2)
Unlawful presence for 3/10 year bars begins accruing from the date authorized stay expires (or entry without inspection). INA § 212(a)(9)(B)(ii)
Manner of Entry — First Entry
Why this is critical
Manner of entry is one of the most consequential facts in an immigration case.

EWI (Entry Without Inspection) bars adjustment of status under § 245(a) because the applicant was never "inspected and admitted or paroled." This eliminates adjustment for most family and employment-based categories. INA § 245(a)

EWI Cures available: (1) § 245(i) grandfathering — if a qualifying petition was filed on or before 4/30/2001, EWI is excused (with $1,000 penalty). INA § 245(i) (2) VAWA exception — VAWA-based adjustment explicitly exempts EWI. INA § 245(a) VAWA carve-out (3) Advance parole cure — if AP is obtained, client departs, and returns as parolee, EWI is cured. 8 CFR § 245.2; Matter of Arrabally, 25 I&N Dec. 771 (BIA 2012)

Unlawful presence bars — EWI triggers unlawful presence from the date of entry, not from expiration of authorized stay. Departure after 180+ days triggers 3-year bar; after 1 year triggers 10-year bar. INA § 212(a)(9)(B)

Note: These bars ONLY trigger on departure. Remaining in the US and adjusting without leaving avoids them entirely.
Visa Type (if entered with visa)
Why we ask
Visa type determines the terms of authorized stay, any bars to adjustment, and grounds of inadmissibility potentially applicable. Key flags:

J-1 visa holders with 2-year home residence requirement must fulfill the requirement or obtain a waiver before changing to most other statuses. INA § 212(e)
F-1 students who fall out of status cannot typically adjust through family (§ 245(c)(2) bars adjustment for those who failed to maintain status). § 245(i) or departure for consular processing may be required. INA § 245(c)(2)
K-1 fiancé(e) visa holders must marry the petitioning USC within 90 days — failure to do so renders the person deportable. INA § 101(a)(15)(K); 8 CFR § 214.2(k)
Has the client made multiple entries to the US?
Why we ask
Multiple entries create several issues:

Continuous presence for cancellation may be "broken" by absences of more than 90 days (single) or 180 days (aggregate). INA § 240A(d)(2)
Prior removal + reentry — if client was previously removed and reentered without inspection, this is a potential aggravated felony under 8 USC § 1326, creating severe criminal and immigration consequences. INA § 241(a)(5); 8 USC § 1326
Reinstatement of removal — a prior removal order can be reinstated upon a subsequent EWI, eliminating most forms of relief. INA § 241(a)(5); 8 CFR § 241.8
Date of Most Recent Entry
Estimated total time in the United States (years)
Why we ask
Total time in US is used to evaluate:

Non-LPR Cancellation — 10 years continuous physical presence required. Absences matter; see § 240A(d)(2). INA § 240A(b)(1)(A)
Naturalization — 5 years as LPR (3 years if married to USC), with continuous residence and physical presence requirements. INA § 316(a); INA § 319(a)
Equities for discretionary relief — length of residence is a major positive equity in any discretionary relief analysis.

Current & Prior Immigration Status

Current Immigration Status
Why we ask
Current status determines: (1) which grounds of removal apply (§ 212 inadmissibility vs. § 237 deportability); (2) whether client is in removal proceedings; (3) what threshold has been crossed for unlawful presence bars.

LPR status triggers § 237 deportability grounds (not § 212 inadmissibility) for conduct occurring after admission. INA § 237(a)
Undocumented / overstay — unlawful presence accruing; departure will trigger 3 or 10-year bar. INA § 212(a)(9)(B)
TPS holders can travel with advance parole and return in parolee status — potentially curing EWI for adjustment. INA § 244; Ramirez v. Brown, 852 F.3d 954 (9th Cir. 2017)
Date LPR Status Obtained
Why we ask
LPR date is the starting point for:

Naturalization — 5-year LPR residency requirement (3 years for spousal). INA § 316(a); § 319(a)
LPR cancellation — 7 years continuous residence and 5 years as LPR required. INA § 240A(a)
§ 212(c) relief — available for pleas entered before April 24, 1996 for LPRs with 7+ years lawful domicile. Former INA § 212(c); INS v. St. Cyr, 533 U.S. 289 (2001)
How was LPR status obtained?
Has the client ever had any prior lawful status that has since expired or been lost?
Why we ask
Prior status that lapsed creates unlawful presence from the date it expired. The exact date of status expiration determines when the 3/10 year bars' clock began. Also relevant:

Authorized stay vs. date-certain — those admitted for "duration of status" (D/S — e.g., F-1, J-1) only begin accruing unlawful presence after a formal USCIS or immigration judge finding that status was violated, not automatically on visa expiration. INA § 212(a)(9)(B)(ii); 8 CFR § 214.1(l)
Has the client ever had a petition filed on their behalf (I-130, I-140, labor certification) on or before April 30, 2001?
Why this can be a game-changer
If any qualifying petition (I-130, I-140, or labor certification) was filed on behalf of this person on or before April 30, 2001, they are permanently "grandfathered" and may adjust status regardless of EWI, unlawful presence, or most immigration violations — upon payment of a $1,000 penalty fee. INA § 245(i); LIFE Act Pub. L. 106-553 (2000)

The grandfathering is permanent — it does not expire. The person need not be the ultimate beneficiary of the original petition; any qualifying petition by that date is sufficient.

Action item: For any long-term client with US family, pull FOIA A-file to check for any qualifying petition. Many exist that the client doesn't know about.

Prior Immigration Proceedings & Orders

Has the client ever received a Notice to Appear (NTA)?
Why the NTA is critical
The NTA is the charging document that initiates removal proceedings. It is also the trigger for the stop-time rule — service of a proper NTA stops accrual of continuous physical presence for cancellation of removal. INA § 240A(d)(1)

Pereira / Niz-Chavez bug: An NTA that lacks a specific hearing date and time is defective and does not stop the clock. Pereira v. Sessions, 585 U.S. 198 (2018); Niz-Chavez v. Garland, 593 U.S. 155 (2021)

Furthermore, Niz-Chavez held that notice must be contained in a single document. An NTA without a hearing date, followed later by a separate notice of hearing, is still defective — two documents cannot be combined to make one valid NTA.

Action item: Pull the original NTA and check: (1) does it contain the date, time, and location of the first hearing? (2) Was it served by personal service or mail? If defective, the stop-time rule was never triggered and continuous presence kept accruing — potentially converting an ineligible case into a cancellation case.
Approximate date NTA was served
Did the NTA contain a specific hearing date and time?
The Pereira issue
This is possibly the most important single question in a cancellation case. If the NTA lacked a hearing date/time, the stop-time rule was never triggered, and continuous presence continued accruing until the government serves a valid, complete NTA. Pereira v. Sessions, 585 U.S. 198 (2018)

In practice: NTAs issued in the 1990s and 2000s frequently lacked hearing information because courts scheduled hearings separately after filing. Pull the original NTA document. Look for language like "a date to be calendared" or "TBD" in the hearing date field — that is a defective NTA.
Has there been an order of removal issued?
Why order type matters enormously
In absentia orders are the most attackable type. Under INA § 240(b)(5)(C), an in absentia order can be rescinded:
• At any time — no time limit — if the respondent did not receive proper notice of the hearing.
• Within 180 days if exceptional circumstances caused the failure to appear. INA § 240(b)(5)(C); 8 CFR § 1003.23(b)(4)(ii)

VAWA exception — regardless of order type, a VAWA-based MTR has no time limit and no numerical limitation. INA § 240(c)(7)(C)(iv)

Orders after merits hearing are more difficult to attack — require new evidence or changed law under 8 CFR § 1003.23(b)(3).

Stipulated removal — client waived all rights. Very difficult to reopen; must show coercion, ineffective assistance, or lack of understanding.
Approximate date of removal order
Has the client ever been physically removed / deported from the US?
Why prior removal is severe
A prior removal creates a bar to admission under INA § 212(a)(9)(A):
• 10-year bar after first removal (standard)
• 20-year bar after second removal
• Permanent bar if removed after an aggravated felony conviction INA § 212(a)(9)(A)

To return legally, client must file Form I-212 (Permission to Reapply for Admission). The I-212 standard is discretionary — no statutory list of factors, but BIA has articulated equities. 8 CFR § 212.2

Reentry after removal (EWI) is a federal crime under 8 USC § 1326, potentially an aggravated felony, and triggers reinstatement of the prior removal order without new proceedings. INA § 241(a)(5); 8 USC § 1326

VAWA exception: VAWA self-petitioners may be eligible for a waiver of the § 212(a)(9)(A) bar. INA § 212(a)(9)(C)(iii)
Date of prior removal
Did client reenter the US after that removal?
Reinstatement of removal
If a client with a prior removal order reentered without inspection, immigration authorities may reinstate the prior order without any new hearing. The reinstated order cannot be appealed or reviewed, and bars nearly all forms of relief except withholding/CAT proceedings if fear of return exists. INA § 241(a)(5); 8 CFR § 241.8

The only question the officer must answer before reinstating is: (1) identity, (2) prior order, (3) illegal reentry. No hearing, no IJ.
Has a prior MTR been filed in this case?
Prior MTR and numerical limits
Normally, a party is limited to one motion to reopen per proceeding, filed within 90 days of the final order. INA § 240(c)(7)(A),(C); 8 CFR § 1003.23(b)(1)

VAWA exception: These limits do NOT apply to VAWA-based MTRs. A VAWA MTR can be filed regardless of: (1) how many prior MTRs were filed, and (2) how old the final order is. INA § 240(c)(7)(C)(iv)

In absentia / notice: MTRs based on lack of notice also have no time limit. INA § 240(b)(5)(C)(ii)
Has the case been appealed to the BIA or any federal court?
Why appeal history matters
Prior appeals affect: (1) which court currently has jurisdiction; (2) whether the case can be remanded; (3) res judicata issues on refiled claims.

BIA jurisdiction: Once a BIA decision is issued, jurisdiction moves to the circuit court. A new MTR must be filed with whichever body last had jurisdiction — IJ (8 CFR § 1003.23) or BIA (8 CFR § 1003.2).

Voluntary departure + appeal: If voluntary departure was granted and an appeal was filed, the voluntary departure period is stayed in some circuits. Failure to depart after the BIA rules triggers a 10-year bar on relief. INA § 240B(d); 8 CFR § 1240.26(i)
